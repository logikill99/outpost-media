Why Is There So Much Crypto in F1?

📖 ~8 min read  |  F1 Business  |  Updated 2025

Look at an F1 car from 2021–2023 and you'll see Crypto.com, FTX, Tezos, ByBit, OKX, Bitpanda, Stake, or some other crypto brand on almost every team. It was one of the most visible sponsorship explosions in the sport's history — and one of the most turbulent. Here's why it happened, what went wrong, and where it stands now.

The Convergence: Why Both Industries Wanted Each Other

The relationship between cryptocurrency exchanges/projects and Formula 1 wasn't random. Several forces pushed them together simultaneously around 2020–2022:

F1's new American audience: Drive to Survive on Netflix — launched 2019, exploded during COVID lockdowns — dramatically expanded F1's North American fanbase. The median new F1 fan was exactly who crypto companies were targeting: young, male, tech-adjacent, higher disposable income, tolerant of risk. F1 gave crypto a direct pipeline to this demographic at global scale.

F1's revamped commercial structure: Liberty Media acquired F1 in 2017 and immediately started modernizing its commercial approach. Content rights, social media, digital fan engagement — everything became more accessible and more aggressive. This opened the door for crypto brands that wouldn't have found an easy path into the old F1 commercial structure.

Crypto's 2020–2021 bull run: Bitcoin went from ~$7,000 in early 2020 to ~$67,000 in late 2021. Ethereum, Solana, and exchanges like Crypto.com and FTX had more money than they knew what to do with. Sports sponsorship was the fastest way to establish legitimacy and reach mainstream audiences. F1 was the most globally visible sport that still had significant sponsorship inventory available.

Gambling-adjacent regulation: Many traditional finance platforms face heavy restrictions on sports sponsorship. Crypto existed in a regulatory gray area where it could effectively function as a financial product advertisement without the disclosure requirements that would apply to a bank or brokerage. This regulatory arbitrage made F1 sponsorship particularly attractive.

The FTX Collapse: The Industry's Worst Moment

In November 2022, FTX — the exchange founded by Sam Bankman-Fried and at one point the second-largest crypto exchange in the world — collapsed in a matter of days after a Coindesk report revealed that its affiliated trading firm Alameda Research held most of its assets in FTX's own native token (FTT). A bank run ensued. FTX halted withdrawals. Within a week, it had filed for bankruptcy.

FTX had sponsored the Miami Grand Prix as a title sponsor — the circuit was formally named "Crypto.com Miami Grand Prix presented by FTX" at some points. FTX branding was on the Mercedes cars. Sam Bankman-Fried had been photographed at race events. It was the single most embarrassing moment for crypto in F1 — a major partner turned out to be running what prosecutors called an $8 billion fraud.

The FTX collapse removed approximately $8–10 billion in sponsorship capital from crypto's marketing budget virtually overnight. Every crypto brand in sports immediately faced scrutiny. Teams that had FTX logos quietly disappeared them. Mercedes had to eat significant revenue loss mid-season.

The Regulatory Response

The FTX collapse accelerated regulatory pressure on crypto advertising in sports. Several countries took action:

  • UK: The Advertising Standards Authority ruled that several crypto sponsorship activations were misleading. The FCA (Financial Conduct Authority) began requiring crypto firms advertising in the UK to include specific risk disclosures.
  • EU: MiCA (Markets in Crypto-Assets) regulation, which came into effect in 2024, created new licensing and disclosure requirements for crypto service providers advertising in EU markets.
  • Australia and Singapore: Both markets implemented stricter guidelines around crypto sports sponsorship targeting retail consumers.

For F1 specifically, this meant that some of the looser "exchange brand awareness" deals became harder to execute at scale. Sponsors needed to ensure compliance with the jurisdiction of each race — a significant operational burden for a sport that races in 20+ countries.

What Survived and What Disappeared

The crypto-F1 relationship didn't end — it matured and consolidated. The brands that survived the 2022–2023 shakeout share a few characteristics:

Regulated exchanges: OKX, Bybit, and Crypto.com are licensed in multiple jurisdictions and can credibly claim regulatory compliance. This makes their sponsorship activations legally defensible in most markets where F1 races.

Integrated fan products: Rather than pure brand awareness, the surviving deals tend to involve actual fan products — NFT collectibles, prediction markets, exclusive content. This gives the sponsorship a functional reason to exist beyond "our logo on the car."

Stake/Kick Sauber: The most visible current crypto brand in F1. The online gambling platform Stake (operating under the Kick brand for some markets) effectively bought a team naming rights deal with Sauber, rebranding them to Kick Sauber. This is unusual — team naming rights deals are rare in F1 because the traditional teams protect their identities fiercely. It gives Stake/Kick an extraordinary level of visibility.

The Gambling Angle

A subtlety that often gets missed: many "crypto" F1 sponsors are also gambling or prediction market operators. Stake is explicitly a gambling platform. Several "exchanges" offer leveraged trading products that are functionally similar to sports betting. The crypto branding is partly a way to market what is, effectively, a gambling product in jurisdictions where traditional sports betting advertising faces more restrictions.

This regulatory arbitrage is increasingly noticed by regulators. The UK's gambling advertising restrictions, for instance, apply to gambling products regardless of whether they're denominated in crypto or fiat. How this tension resolves will shape the next phase of crypto-F1 sponsorship.

Current State (2025)

The 2025 F1 grid has fewer crypto sponsors than the 2022 peak, but they're more embedded and more stable:

  • OKX has diversified into multiple sports (football, cricket) and positioned itself as a premium sports technology partner rather than just an exchange.
  • Bybit's Red Bull partnership survived the post-FTX scrutiny and continues.
  • Kick Sauber represents the most aggressive integration — team naming rights with a gambling/crypto brand is something the pre-Liberty F1 would not have allowed.
  • Several NFT-focused partnerships from the 2021 era (Tezos, NEAR) have quietly expired without renewal, as the NFT market collapsed in 2023.

The peak crypto moment in F1 is probably behind us. What remains is a smaller set of more stable relationships with better-capitalized, more regulated brands. Whether the next wave — if crypto has another significant bull cycle — produces another sponsorship explosion remains to be seen. The sport is somewhat more cautious now after the FTX experience, but money talks loudly in a sport where annual budgets run to hundreds of millions.
