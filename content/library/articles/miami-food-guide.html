What to Eat in Miami — A Food Guide

📖 ~8 min read  |  Travel  |  Updated 2025

Miami's food scene is one of the most underrated in the United States. It's not a "city with good restaurants" — it's a city where the food is a direct expression of who actually lives there. Cuban Miami, Haitian Miami, Colombian Miami, Venezuelan Miami, Brazilian Miami — all of them have distinct food cultures and all of them are operating within a few miles of the racing circuit. Here's where to actually eat.

Cuban Food: The Foundation

Cuban cuisine is to Miami what Italian food is to New York — so deeply integrated into the city's identity that it's basically mandatory. The epicenter is Little Havana (Calle Ocho), about 15 miles from the Hard Rock Stadium circuit.

What to order:

  • Ropa vieja: Shredded flank steak braised in tomato, peppers, and olives. The national dish of Cuba. If you eat nothing else, eat this.
  • Lechón asado: Slow-roasted pork, marinated in mojo (citrus + garlic). Usually served with black beans and rice.
  • Medianoche: The midnight sandwich — ham, roasted pork, Swiss cheese, pickles, mustard on a sweet egg bread. Different from the Cubano in bread and ingredient proportions.
  • Cubano: The classic pressed sandwich. Ham, pork, Swiss, pickles, mustard. Every Cuban restaurant has their own version.
  • Café cubano: Espresso sweetened with sugar whipped into the crema. Extremely strong, extremely sweet. Available at window counters across the city for $1–2.
  • Cortadito: Café cubano with a splash of steamed milk. If you can't handle a straight café cubano (you can, but just in case).
  • Croquetas: Ham croquettes. Everywhere. Usually good everywhere. Cheap.

Versailles Restaurant

3555 SW 8th St, Little Havana | Cash-friendly, always open late

The most famous Cuban restaurant in Miami. A Little Havana institution since 1971. The food is consistent, the portions are enormous, and the café cubano at the ventanita (walk-up window) is the reference standard. Go for the window experience even if you don't sit down.

El Cristo Restaurant

1543 SW 8th St, Little Havana | Neighborhood spot

Less touristed than Versailles. Older clientele, more local feel. The ropa vieja here is exceptional. Family-run since the 1960s.

Haitian Food: Little Haiti

Little Haiti sits northeast of downtown, between I-95 and Biscayne Boulevard. Haitian food is criminally underrated in American food culture — it's deeply flavored, historically complex, and represents one of the most distinct Caribbean culinary traditions.

What to order:

  • Griot: Fried pork marinated in citrus and spices. Crispy outside, succulent inside. Often served with pikliz (spicy pickled cabbage) and fried plantains.
  • Pikliz: The Haitian condiment. Pickled cabbage, carrots, scotch bonnets — extremely spicy, acidic, and addictive. Put it on everything.
  • Joumou (Soup Joumou): Pumpkin soup traditionally eaten on Haitian Independence Day (January 1st). Thick, rich, filled with meat and vegetables. Hard to find outside special occasions but worth seeking.
  • Tassot: Jerked goat or beef. Deep flavor, chewy, served with fried plantains.
  • Akra: Malanga root fritters. Crispy, slightly starchy, perfect with pikliz.

Chez Le Bebe Restaurant

114 NE 54th St, Little Haiti | Cash only, tiny

The most famous Haitian restaurant in Miami. Small, no-frills, perfect food. The griot plate is the standard order. Cash only, and they may run out of food by mid-afternoon — go early.

South American: The Brickell / Doral Circuit

Miami has one of the largest Venezuelan diaspora populations in the United States (Doral, sometimes called "Doralzuela," is approximately 70% Venezuelan). Colombian and Peruvian food are also well-represented.

Venezuelan highlights:

  • Arepas: Cornmeal flatbreads filled with everything — shredded chicken (reina pepiada), black beans and cheese (dominó), shredded beef. Better in Miami than most other US cities because the population density has raised the quality floor.
  • Pabellón criollo: The Venezuelan national dish. Black beans, white rice, shredded beef, fried plantains. Four simple things together that are better than any single element.
  • Cachapas: Sweet corn pancakes folded over fresh white cheese. Breakfast or snack. Absolutely worth finding.

Seafood: Miami Does This Right

Miami is a coastal city with year-round warm water access and significant fishing industry. The seafood here is fresh in a way that landlocked cities can't match.

  • Stone crab claws: Florida stone crabs are sustainably harvested (only the claws are taken, the crab regenerates and is returned to the water). Available October–May. The claws are served chilled with mustard sauce. Joe's Stone Crab in South Beach is the original and still excellent, but pricey and requires reservation or serious wait.
  • Grouper: Florida's signature fish. Sweet, firm, versatile. Best grilled or fried. Available at nearly every seafood restaurant in the metro.
  • Mahi-mahi: Abundant in Florida waters. Meaty, mild, holds up well to grilling. Often served in fish tacos.
  • Conch fritters: Conch is a Caribbean staple — the meat is pounded and fried in fritters with peppers and onions. More common in the Keys but findable in Miami.

Garcia's Seafood Grille & Fish Market

398 NW North River Dr, Downtown | Water views, casual

Genuine working waterfront seafood. The fish market is attached to the restaurant — they're selling what came off the boats. Grouper sandwich here is one of the best in the city. Cash bar, cash menu, bring cash.

Wynwood: Where Chefs Experiment

Wynwood has become Miami's food innovation district over the past decade, alongside the street art scene. The restaurants here tend toward experimentation — fusion concepts, tasting menus, modern takes on Caribbean and Latin flavors.

The neighborhood is particularly good for:

  • Modern Venezuelan fusion (arepas elevated to restaurant dishes)
  • Peruvian-Japanese fusion (Nikkei cuisine)
  • Craft cocktail programs with Latin spirits (Rum, Cachaça, Mezcal)
  • Brunch spots with elaborate weekend menus
Near the circuit: Hard Rock Stadium is in Miami Gardens — less culinary density than Wynwood, Brickell, or Little Havana. For race weekend eating, either eat at the circuit (usually good food options at the F1 paddock level), or Uber to Little Havana or Wynwood in the evenings. The drive from the circuit to Little Havana is about 20 minutes off-peak, significantly longer during race weekend congestion.

Craft Beer and Drinks

Miami has developed a legitimate craft beer scene in the last decade:

  • Wynwood Brewing: The original Wynwood craft brewery. La Rubia blonde ale is their flagship. Taproom is casual and always busy on weekends.
  • J. Wakefield Brewing: More experimental — heavy stouts, sours, barrel-aged beers. In Wynwood, lines on can release days.
  • Biscayne Bay Brewing: Bay-side location, lighter style beers suited to Miami's heat. More approachable for non-craft-beer people.

For rum: Miami's proximity to Caribbean rum culture means the rum programs at better bars tend to be exceptional. Look for aged Barbadian and Jamaican rums and Haitian Clairin (an unaged agricole-style spirit) at higher-end cocktail bars.

What Not to Do

  • Don't eat at the hotel restaurant if you're staying near the circuit. Unless it's an emergency.
  • Don't avoid Little Havana because it "looks sketchy." It's a historic neighborhood with remarkable food.
  • Don't overlook the dollar windows (ventanitas) for Cuban coffee and croquetas — the best café cubano in Miami costs $1.50.
  • Don't miss the local fruit vendors. Miami's produce culture includes tropical fruits you will not find outside Florida — mamey sapote, canistel, carambola, black sapote. Worth seeking out at Sedano's or a local market.

Miami rewards exploration. The food here isn't in the Michelin star restaurants (though there are some) — it's in the family spots that have been running the same recipe for 40 years, the beach shacks, the strip mall Haitian restaurants, the ventanita counters with three people ordering in Spanish. Get away from the circuit and eat.
