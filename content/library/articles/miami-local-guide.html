Miami — A Nerd's Guide to South Florida

Category: Travel · Reading time: ~6 min · Written for Outpost

You're in Miami for the F1. But there's an interesting city around you, if you know where to look. This is the technically-minded visitor's guide to South Florida — what it is, how it works, and what's worth your time.

The Geography Problem: Miami is Not One Place

The most common tourist confusion about Miami is the geography. "Miami" refers to several distinct municipalities that outsiders often conflate:

  • City of Miami: The actual city proper, centered around Brickell and Downtown. Dense, increasingly vertical, skyscraper finance district, Wynwood arts district, Little Havana.
  • Miami Beach: A separate barrier island municipality connected by causeways. Art Deco hotels, South Beach, the actual beach. Not Miami.
  • Miami Gardens: Where you are. A separate city north of Miami, incorporated in 2003 — the largest majority-Black city in Florida. Hard Rock Stadium is here, as is FIU's north campus.
  • Coral Gables: Southwest Miami-Dade, home of the University of Miami. Mediterranean Revival architecture, old money, very manicured.
  • Hialeah: West of Miami, one of the most Cuban cities in America — English is genuinely optional there.

Miami-Dade County is the umbrella jurisdiction. "Greater Miami" is the metropolitan area. The Miami metro has ~6 million people. The City of Miami proper has about 450,000.

Cuban Culture and the Miami Identity

Miami's distinctive character comes primarily from Cuban immigration, which fundamentally transformed the city starting with the 1959 revolution. The first wave of Cuban exiles were largely professional and middle-class — doctors, lawyers, engineers — who rebuilt their lives in Miami and created an economic and cultural foundation that subsequent waves built upon.

Today, the Miami-Dade metro is majority Hispanic (roughly 70%), with Cuban-Americans as the single largest group. This has tangible effects:

  • Spanish is genuinely co-equal to English as a functional language. Not just in restaurants — in business, government, and daily life.
  • Cuban food is not "Mexican food with extra steps" — it's a distinct cuisine. If you get the opportunity: Cuban sandwich (pressed, with Swiss, ham, roasted pork, pickles, mustard — not the Miami Beach tourist version), ropa vieja, picadillo, croquetas. Get coffee from a ventanita (walk-up window) and drink it standing up like a person.
  • The political orientation of Cuban-Miami is complex — historically strongly Republican due to anti-communism, but increasingly varied across generations and newer immigrant groups.

The Weather System

May in Miami is entering the wet season. What this means practically:

  • Temperature: 28–33°C (82–91°F) during the day. High humidity. Feels hotter.
  • Afternoon thunderstorms: Miami's classic pattern is clear mornings, building cumulus by noon, thunderstorms 2–5pm, clearing by evening. These can be severe — lightning, heavy rain, high winds. They pass quickly.
  • Hurricane season: Officially June 1. May is pre-season but not immune. The circuit has a wet weather GP option but it typically doesn't get used.
  • Recommendation: Hydrate aggressively. More than you think. The combination of heat, humidity, and alcohol at the race has a compounding effect that hits you around lap 30.

The Tech Scene: Magic City Innovation District

Miami made a significant push as a tech hub starting around 2020–2021, when Mayor Francis Suarez's viral "How can I help?" tweet responding to tech investors looking to leave San Francisco genuinely caught attention. The subsequent migration wasn't as massive as the headlines suggested, but it was real.

Wynwood (the arts district, north of downtown) has become the primary tech cluster — co-working spaces, startup offices, and VCs mixed in with the street art galleries. The Magic City Innovation District, currently under development in the Little River neighborhood, is an attempt to build a deliberate tech campus. It's a genuine work in progress.

The honest assessment: Miami has the lifestyle advantages (weather, no state income tax, international connectivity) but lacks the density of talent and capital of SF or NYC. It's a good satellite office location and a reasonable place for post-Series-A companies to put an East Coast hub. It's not going to replace Silicon Valley. The startup ecosystem is real but early-stage.

Florida's No State Income Tax

This is real and significant. Florida has no personal income tax. Combined with the tax situation of certain business structures, this is a meaningful financial consideration for high earners. It's a large part of why Brickell has become a finance hub, with hedge funds and family offices relocating from New York and Connecticut.

University of Miami

UM (locally called "The U," not to be confused with the Florida Gators' "The Swamp") is a private research university in Coral Gables, founded 1925. Strong programs in marine science (proximity to the ocean is an obvious asset), law, music (Frost School of Music is excellent), business, and engineering. Research university with R1 status. Football is the culturally dominant sport on campus — UM had a historically significant dynasty in the 1980s-early 2000s that produced an absurd number of NFL players.

What's Worth Your Time

Wynwood Walls

The Wynwood Walls are an outdoor museum of large-scale murals in a formerly industrial neighborhood. Started by Tony Goldman in 2009, it catalyzed the transformation of Wynwood into Miami's arts district. The walls feature work by international street artists — names like Shepard Fairey, Os Gemeos, KAWS. Even if you're not an art person, the concentration of high-quality work in a walkable area is genuinely impressive.

Brickell and the Financial District

Brickell is Miami's Manhattan — dense high-rises, walkable (Miami's most walkable area), lots of good restaurants and bars. The Brickell City Centre is a massive mixed-use development that integrated climate-controlled pedestrian bridges — an acknowledgment that Miami's heat makes outdoor walking legitimately unpleasant in summer. Worth a walk through.

Little Havana

The cultural heart of Cuban Miami. Calle Ocho (SW 8th Street) is the main drag. Older than you might expect — it's not a tourist recreation, it's a living neighborhood that's been there for sixty years. The Domino Park (Máximo Gómez Park) has older Cuban men playing dominoes daily. Get coffee. Try the food. It's genuinely different from the Miami Beach tourist experience.

The Beaches

Miami Beach has some of the best urban beach access in the US — wide sand, warm water, accessible transit. The Art Deco Historic District on Ocean Drive has well-preserved 1930s-40s architecture. South Pointe (south end of Miami Beach) is calmer and more local. If you want to swim: the water in May is around 27°C, no wetsuit needed.

Practical Notes

  • Getting around: Miami is very car-centric. The Metrorail is limited but covers downtown/Brickell/Coconut Grove well. Uber/Lyft pricing surges significantly on GP weekend. Plan accordingly.
  • Internet: You presumably know how this works or you wouldn't be using this server. Just don't rely on cell service in the stadium footprint.
  • Nightlife: Genuinely world-class if that's your thing. Late-starting by US standards — nothing is really happening before midnight. The hospitality economy is large and competitive.
  • Tipping culture: Standard US service industry expectations. 20% is the baseline. Some venues add service charges automatically — check your bill before adding more.

Miami is weirder and more interesting than its Instagram version suggests. The culture is genuinely Latin American in a way that most US cities aren't. The tech scene is younger and less ossified than the coasts. The food is legitimately excellent if you get off the tourist strip. Enjoy it.
